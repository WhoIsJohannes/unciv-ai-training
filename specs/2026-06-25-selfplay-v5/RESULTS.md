# RESULTS — selfplay-v5: Continual Training

**Headline: YES — not resetting clears the blind baseline.** Continual training (warm-start net +
carried Adam state across rounds) takes the structured GNN from v4's **23.0%** to **40.7%** on Medium,
**beating v4 from-scratch (p=6.5e-05)** and **clearing the blind baseline 28.9% (p=0.0069)** — same
encoder, same small rung, same per-round budget as v4. The only change is the training regime.

All evals: Medium map, 200-game ceiling, seed **4242424**, opponent = fixed RandomPolicy, turn-cap 250.
z-tests one-sided via `analyze._two_proportion_z`. Baselines (fixed, from the v4 run): v4
structured-from-scratch small rung = **47/204 (23.0%)**; v2 blind-critic = **58/200 (28.9%)**.

### Regime vs. round-count — the honest disentangling (council FND-0029)
v5 changed two things vs v4: the regime (continual) AND the round count (8→16). To separate them, the
matched comparison is v5 **at round 7** (its 8th round — exactly where v4 stopped):

- ARM A at round 7 (80g): **19.0%** — *below* v4's 23.0%. **At matched round count, continual is NOT
  ahead.**
- ARM A final ceiling (16 rounds, 200g): **40.7%**.

So the win is **not** "continual is better per-round early." It is that continual training **accumulates
skill across rounds** — the climb to 38–49% happens in rounds 8–15, the rounds v4's reset-every-round
regime could never reach. Continual training's value is *compounding*; it needs the extra rounds to
show, and v4 structurally could not use them. (The 80g per-round evals are noisier than the 200g
ceiling — read the trajectory, not single points; ARM B in particular oscillates more at the
higher-capacity rung.)

## Micro-batching (AC3) — PASS
The medium rung trained on Medium (maxn≈1261) for all 16 rounds with `--micro-batch-steps 256` and
**no OOM** — the gate that forced v4 down to the small rung is gone. It is also the strongest arm
(46.6% ceiling): added capacity pays off *once continual training lets it accumulate*. The micro-batch
path is numerically equivalent to the whole-batch path (size-weighted chunk accumulation; verified by
`test_microbatch.py` — loss within 1e-5, grad-norm within 1e-4, post-step weights within 1e-5), and the
small-rung primary arm ran with micro-batch as a **no-op** (byte-identical to the v4 traversal), keeping
it apples-to-apples.

## Throughput (AC4) — PASS

onnx_tps=214.8, **ratio=1.488 ≥ 0.70 → PASS** (13.6 ms/decision). Warm-start does not change inference

## Determinism / provenance / legality (AC5) — preserved
ONNX export contract + Kotlin bridge **byte-unchanged** (no contract bump; `export_rich` neighbors path,
contract_version=3; `OnnxPolicy.kt`/`SampleSchema.kt` untouched). Eval ran through the frozen
provenance gates (ruleset_fingerprint + schema_version); masked heads → zero illegal actions
(onnx_decisions counted every eval); seeds reproduce (gen `gen_seed+r*1000`, fixed eval seed; the
warm-start curve is deterministic given the round-0 seed because `manual_seed` runs only on the fresh
round-0 branch).

## PPO correctness (AC6) — preserved, and slightly improved
The frozen per-update math (`_optimize_actor_critic` 144–192: GAE V-snapshot, importance-ratio clip,
value MSE, entropy, combined objective, grad-clip 10.0) is byte-verbatim; only the optimizer
construction (lifted out + injected) and the forward **traversal** (micro-batch chunking) changed.
Numerical equivalence of the chunked path is tested. Warm-start is fully on-policy: each round still
trains only on data generated by the prior round's exported ONNX, and `old_logp` is recomputed from the
warm net — whose weights ARE the prior round's exported gen weights (verified by
`test_continual_resume.py::test_ac6_warm_net_matches_exported_onnx`, logits within 1e-4). In fact this
is *more* on-policy-correct than v4, where `old_logp` came from a freshly-random-initialized net rather
than the behavior policy.

## Reward + heads (AC7) — unchanged
Terminal-only ±1 reward unchanged; heads exactly {tech, policy} + a train-only tanh(value) head dropped
at export (asserted by `test_gae::test_export_drops_value_head` + the AC6 out-name check).

## Replay — DEFERRED
A cross-round replay buffer is NOT PPO-correct without behavior-policy log-probs at collection (the
recorder writes only the chosen int index; `old_logp` is recomputed in-round, valid only because each
round's data is on-policy). Making replay correct requires recording behavior logp in the Kotlin
recorder + shard schema — out of v5's scope. **Gated on a future logp-recording step.** Warm-start
itself needs no replay (each round trains on its own freshly-generated on-policy batch).

## Bottom line
The v4 diagnosis was right: the encoder was never the bottleneck — the **from-scratch-per-round
training regime** was. Making training continual (warm-start weights + carried Adam state, run long
enough to accumulate) clears the blind baseline for the first time. Next step (out of v5 scope): record
behavior log-probs to unlock a correct cross-round replay buffer, and/or add the heuristic-only action
heads (units/cities/great-person/vote).
